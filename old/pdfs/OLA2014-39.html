  THE POP-UP LIBRARY

Chantal Phillips, Assistant Librarian, 
University of Guelph Ridgetown

Creating awareness of library services online and in print for 
a new cohort of diploma students each year is a challenge. 
The Pop-Up Library was designed to reach out to students 
that don’t make it into the campus library. The portable 
book cart, laptop, reference desk, and library staffer offered 
lunch hour service within the Veterinary Technology 
building. This was modelled on successful pop-up library 
experiences in Northern Europe.

  WHAT LIBRARIANS SHOULD KNOW ABOUT 

ONLINE BEHAVIOURAL TRACKING

Jacquelyn Burkell, Associate Professor, Assistant Dean (Research), 
Western University; Alexandre Fortier, LIS PhD Graduate Student

This poster will explore online behavioural tracking, using 
examples from our study on behavioural tracking on 
consumer health information websites. Poster will include 
associated privacy risks, identify the range of tracking 
mechanisms in use, examine technologies and tools that 
help users identify the presence of tracking measures, 
examine tracking on a selection of websites, and discuss 
disclosure of these practices in website privacy policies.

  UNDERSTANDING GRADUATE  

STUDENT NEEDS: IMPLICATIONS  
FOR UNIVERSITY LIBRARY SERVICES

Afra Bolefski, Business & Economics Librarian, Wilfrid Laurier 
University; Mark Weiler, MLIS Candidate, Western University

In recent years, Ontario universities have looked to 
increase graduate students enrolment. To support this 
population growth, university libraries must develop 
public services to meet the needs of graduate students. 
Drawing on staged-based theories of graduate student 
retention, this poster explains how student needs vary 
across three stages (transition, development, research). 
Libraries may better help graduate students by providing 
services that meet their needs at each of these stages. This 
poster then presents the results of an analysis of graduate 
public services advertised on 10 Ontario university library 
websites.

  A LIBRARIAN’S DREAM COME TRUE –  

DATA ACCESS MADE EFFORTLESS

Alexandra Cooper, Queen’s University; Jane Fry, 
Data Specialist, Carleton University

A collaborative effort among the Ontario data community 
created ODESI (Ontario Data Documentation, Extraction 
Service, and Infrastructure), a data portal. This 
revolutionary process of obtaining data has transformed 
data access for researchers, empowering them by providing 
ready access to this vital library service. This poster 
illustrates the cumbersome process that was necessary to 
obtain data 15 years ago and compares it with the quick, 
innovative way it is obtained today, thus freeing up the 
librarian’s time to educate the researcher in the intricacies 
of data literacy.

  WIN: PROMOTING LIFELONG LITERACY

Ferelith Hoffmann-Taylor, Tobermory Public 
Library Friends of the Library

“We will positively impact women and families within our 
community.” With this vision, the Women’s Information 
Network (WIN) endeavors to provide access to lifelong 
learning for women within our community. Along with 
this we promote the feeling of belonging to the Tobermory 
Community where we live. We developed our Mission 
Statement, Vision, Values, and a Strategy in 2007 – 
beginning with only a few women and have grown to over 
165 within six years. Our poster will discuss how we did this 
and how it could work in other smaller communities.

  “LIBRARY CATALOGUE” TO “RESEARCH PORTAL”: 

MODERNIZING FOR BETTER SERVICE DELIVERY

Kathryn Tippell-Smith, Research Analyst, 
Ontario Medical Association

With ever-increasing strides in technological development 
and the changing needs and expectations of library users, 
the Ontario Medical Association’s Research Group opted 
to migrate from our antiquated integrated library system to 
a modern “social libraries” system, that has revolutionized 
how we provide services to users. This poster details 
the trials, tribulations, and successes of developing our 
“Research Portal” – including planning and preparing 
data, working with the vendor during initial customization, 
the “migration” itself (communicating with our local 
information technology department), and preparing and 
evaluating new delivery methods for our services.

  POCKET REFERENCE:  

THE ASKON TEXT PILOT PROGRAM

Lauren Bourdages, Text Intern; Stacey Nordlund, Text Intern; askON

askON, a collaboration of public and college libraries in 
Ontario, provides a real-time chat information service that 
offers immediate, interactive, and knowledgeable online 
research/reference help. From September to December 
2013, askON ran a text message-based virtual reference 
pilot program alongside their regular chat program, with 
the participation of 14 of their 28 partners (eight college, six 
public). The Pocket Reference poster session presents the 
past, present, and future of this pilot program, articulating 
its origins and staffing; analyzing the data from the four 
month pilot; and exploring its future.
