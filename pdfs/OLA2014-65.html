  THE POWER OF AARON SWARTZ’S  

STORY IN FIRST-YEAR SEMINARS

Paula Hannaford, Undergraduate Experience & Assessment 
Librarian, University of Toronto Mississauga

My research leave on the use of storytelling in teaching 
resulted in a proposal to integrate Aaron Swartz’s 
(computer genius and political activist) story into our 
library’s programming for first-year student seminars within 
their transition programs (commuter, residence, and first 
generation). As the co-ordinator of the programming, I 
developed content for the training and seminars, co-taught 
with student-peer mentors and a team of librarians in fall 
2013. The poster will outline the process and share the 
empowering results of interactive sessions, conversations 
about research, open access, and more.

  INFORMATION USE & REUSE:  

COMMUNITY COLLABORATION  
WITH OPEN-SOURCE WEB MAPPING

Kim Pham, Student, University of Toronto

Open-source technologies are creating new avenues 
for community engagement. Web-developer tools are 
empowering librarians and users alike. This project utilizes 
open-source GIS and JavaScript libraries to visualize data 
from the Scarborough Historical Society photo collection 
in an interactive app. Ongoing developments will invite 
users to contribute their own stories, comments, and 
pictures that build community history. The success of this 
project led to its adoption as a teaching and literacy tool 
in the Scarborough Archives. Learn the challenges and 
considerations for integrating open-source tools for service 
use.

 READERS’ ADVISORY IN ACADEMIC LIBRARIES

Gwen Fisher, Oakville PL; Kate Restivo, Toronto PL; 
Patterson Jordan, University of Toronto; Matthew Rohweder, 
Student & Reference Staff, Faculty of Information; Keren Dali, 
Faculty of Information & Media Studies, Western University

This poster, resulting from student–professor 
collaboration, presents the teaching methods of Readers’ 
Advisory (RA) in academia and examples of student work 
geared toward integrating leisure reading into reference 
and information literacy practices of academic libraries. 
RA used to be a common practice in academic libraries 
in the 1930s–1940s, but then it relocated exclusively to 
public libraries. In the last 10 years, RA in academia has 
experienced revival, as educators have become increasingly 
aware of the importance of non-academic reading for 
student performance and development. In the last three 
years, RA in academia has been taught as part of the RA 
course at the iSchool, University of Toronto.

  SYSTEMATIC REVIEWS &  

THE EVOLVING ROLE OF LIBRARIANS

Shannon Gordon, Liaison Librarian, Davis Centre 
Library & School of Pharmacy; Rebecca Hutchinson, 
Liaison Librarian, Kinesiology, Optometry & Public 
Health & Health Systems; University of Waterloo

The demand for systematic reviews (SR) in research 
intensive health-related departments is rapidly increasing, 
and academic librarians have the expertise necessary to 
support these comprehensive reviews. In summer 2013, 
three University of Waterloo health librarians surveyed 
faculty on their current and future systematic review 
work. Through this process we determined researcher 
expectations of librarian support and identified multiple 
ways to meet their needs. Our poster will illustrate possible 
librarian roles in the systematic review process, and how 
our expertise can be used towards knowledge creation.

  THE IMPACT OF LIS FIELD  

EXPERIENCES ON CAREER SUCCESS

Judith Logan, Reference Librarian, Robarts Library 
Reference & Research Services, University of Toronto

Field experiences are often cited as one of the best ways 
for LIS students to improve their chances of getting a 
post-graduation job. Anecdotal evidence corroborates this 
advice, but there is little in the literature to support LIS 
students’ decision making. What kinds of field experiences 
have the most impact? How important is it to have a field 
experience in the same type of library as you plan to pursue 
a career in? How does geography fit in? This poster will 
report preliminary findings of a mixed-methods study that 
is attempting to answer these questions.
