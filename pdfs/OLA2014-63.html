  SUMMON NOW & THEN: STUDENT SATISFACTION 

TWO YEARS ON

Kevin Manuel, Data Librarian; Dana Thomas, Evaluation 
& Assessment Librarian; Ryerson University

Many academic libraries today invest heavily in Web Scale 
Discovery services to meet the needs and expectations 
of undergraduate students. Now that these services have 
been in use for a few years, has the initial excitement 
waned? Do students still feel they have “won the lottery”? 
The presenters will share the results of two survey 
iterations, comparing satisfaction levels with the service 
in 2013–2014 with those evident in 2011–2012. We will 
show how students at Ryerson University like using Search 
Everything, powered by Summon.

  LEVEL UP! DESIGNING A GAMING LAB AT 

CARLETON UNIVERSITY LIBRARY

Emma Cross, Cataloguing Librarian;  
Robert Smith, Subject Specialist Computer Science, 
Electronics & Systems Engineering; Carleton University

In December 2013 Carleton University Library unveiled 
an extensively renovated building including a new library 
gaming lab, part of a digital Discovery Centre. Carleton 
University is now the only academic library in Ontario 
with a collection of over 500 video games and a uniquely 
equipped gaming lab. This poster discusses planning a 
library gaming lab, illustrates the equipment and services 
needed by faculty and students and analyzes how this 
innovative library service is integral in supporting courses 
in information technology, computer science, digital 
humanities, and other courses across campus.

  CORE JOURNALS IN CANADIAN ABORIGINAL 

STUDIES – ACADEMIC LIBRARY PERSPECTIVES

Kirsten Huhn, Head, Electronic Resources & Receiving, Concordia 
University; Michelle Lake, Gov Docs, Poli Sci Librarian

Aboriginal/Indigenous/Native/First Peoples studies are 
a growing field in academic intuitions across Canada. 
Academic libraries need to respond by providing the best 
possible scholarly resources to our communities. Our 
research focuses on identifying core journals in Canadian 
aboriginal studies, to enhance collection development, 
and research in this discipline. We hope to create a list of 
core journals in Canadian Aboriginal Studies by comparing 
collections at different academic libraries, compiling 
an inventory of current publications in the field, and 
investigating what journals are faculty’s preferred venues 
for publishing their research.

  HISTORY CLUB: AN ARCHIVAL OUTREACH 

PROGRAM TO HIGH SCHOOL STUDENTS

Leigh Jackson, Program Liaison, Library Resource Centre, Lambton 
County Archives; Heather Lavallee, Student Assistant, Faculty 
of Information & Media Studies Graduate Resource Centre

History Club is an outreach initiative taken on by the 
Lambton County Archives. The program came about thanks 
to a partnership with teachers at a local high school. The 
archivist acted as a consultant, helping students research 
fallen soldiers for the Lest We Forget Project. Through a 
series of presentations and the creation of a dedicated 
research website, we are able to support the student’s work 
while connecting with teenagers – a previously under-
represented user group. The student’s work will also be 
added to our archives for community use.

  ANATOMY OF PURE & HYBRID RDA BIB RECORDS

Beverley Ball, Cataloguer, Collections Services 
Department; John Cooper, Senior Cataloguer, 
Collections Services Department; London PL

Cataloguers in Ontario cope with the challenge of AACR2 
(Anglo-American Cataloging Rules 2) changing to RDA 
(Resource Description and Access). In this poster, we will 
demonstrate our approach, an evolving hybrid style. A pure 
RDA record will be contrasted with our hybrid style. Labels 
on various “body parts” will call attention to changes. Flaps 
will lift to reveal LPL thinking about each element. We hope 
that this will empower other libraries to embark on the road 
toward RDA records.

  PROMOTING GEO-LITERACY  

WITH OPEN DATA & ONLINE MAPPING TOOLS

Tanya Kenesky, GIS/Data Technician; Andrew Nicholson, 
GIS, Data Librarian; University of Toronto Mississauga

In recent years, open-data initiatives have swept across 
Canadian cities with many now releasing samples of their 
locally-collected geospatial and numeric datasets for public 
use. Along with these developments, powerful and freely 
available online mapping software has become available 
allowing for innovative opportunities for GIS education and 
promoting geo-literacy. This poster will highlight one such 
case at the University of Toronto Mississauga, in which 
the library crafted an online “Taste of GIS” tutorial for 
Environmental Studies students.
