  SEARCHING THE DEEP WEB

Lei Jin, Electronic Resources Librarian; 
Jay Wolofsky, Librarian; Ryerson University

The Deep Web or the Invisible Web contains high quality 
information found in more specialized or remote corners 
of the Internet. The Deep Web is currently 400 to 500 
times larger than the commonly defined World Wide Web 
and contains 550 billion individual documents compared 
to one billion in the surface web. Standard search engines 
like Google cannot retrieve content from the Deep Web. 
Rather, federated search engines execute simultaneous 
real-time searches of the Deep Web using sophisticated 
software “connectors”. Results are collated and presented 
to the user in a unified format. This workshop will include 
live demos of federated search engines covering science, 
business, nursing, etc.

  DETERMINING THE DIRECTION OF AN 

INFORMATION LITERACY PROGRAM:  
DATA ANALYSIS LEADS TO THE  
CREATION OF ONLINE-LEARNING OBJECTS

Mara Bordignon; Kathryn Klages, Information Literacy 
& Liaison Librarian; Gail Strachan, Information 
Literacy & Liaison Librarian; Seneca College

In the Fall of 2010 Seneca Libraries started to collect 
detailed data on the type, length, and preparation time 
of every information literacy activity undertaken in 
the library in order to assess the breadth and depth of 
our information literacy program; to identify program 
areas within the College that we were not systematically 
reaching and to ensure all first-year students received 
basic information literacy skills as mandated in our 
academic plan. In analyzing the data we discovered we 
were teaching face-to-face only one of every four students 
enrolled in College English. The large number of programs 
and courses indicated that it would not be feasible to 
instruct all students face-to-face. We determined we 
could meet our objective to ensuring all students receive 
information literacy instruction through the development 
and implementation of online learning modules. We have 
developed over 12 online modules with assessments to 
date, with more in development.

  WHAT’S HEALTH GOT TO DO WITH IT?  

SICKKIDS READS!

Jennifer Borkowski, Children’s Librarian, The Hospital for Sick 
Children; Kathy Anderson, Teacher-Librarian, Toronto DSB

The Hospital for Sick Children and the Toronto District 
School Board has a unique partnership in the mandate to 
promote literacy in an acute care setting. This poster will 
highlight the education program, book lending Children’s 
Library, mobile library service, and edutainment activities 
that promote literacy in developing minds. Other hospital 
programs include promoting reading to babies, storytimes, 
and reading clubs in collaboration with the Therapeutic 
Clown Program, Child Life Specialists, and multi-
disciplinary health care team.

  BEYOND ROCK-PAPER-SCISSORS:  

CHOOSING FRAMEWORKS  
TO ALIGN OUTCOMES WITH TEACHING

Saira Mall, Educational Technology Liaison; Joanna Szurmak, 
Science Librarian; University of Toronto Mississauga

Instructional sessions, whether embedded or one-shot, 
need to have learning outcomes that align activities with 
teaching methods. While it is our ideal to develop skills 
identified through learning outcomes, often the focus is 
on delivering content. In order to shift away from content-
driven sessions and towards course goals, we need to 
establish a learning-focused interaction when consulting 
with faculty. In this poster we will connect learning-focused 
conversation techniques developed by Lipton & Wellman 
(2003) to Shank & Dewald’s (2003) Micro and Macro 
model of library presence in courses.

  GROWING A SUPPORT PROGRAM  

FOR THE NEW BLENDED SERVICES  
MODEL AT M

c

MASTER UNIVERSITY

Jill Bedford, Library Assistant, McMaster University; 
Rodney Howland, Library Assistant, Innis Library

Launched in 2009, MAC’s Blended Services Model 
demanded more complex skills from library assistants 
at main service points. This led to the development of a 
library training committee composed of a blended services 
staff member from each library and a librarian. This group 
developed and implemented a participatory training plan 
for ongoing staff support. The poster will showcase a 
unique combination of training strategies and diverse 
activities that led to the successful implementation of this 
initiative. Two years later, results from surveys and feedback 
show the model works.
