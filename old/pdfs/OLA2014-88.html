Older adults

Where is the GPS 
for Eldercare?

Dr. Michael Gordon, 
Medical Program 
Director, Palliative Care, 
Baycrest, Geriatric 
Health Care System

Canada’s population is aging. The challenge 
to health care providers and elders’ families 
is profound. The major source of support and 
navigation of the health care system is the family. If 
only there were a GPS for this. Since there isn’t one, 
the key to success is knowledge and understanding 
of the issues.
Dr. Gordon is American born, educated at the 
University of St. Andrews Medical School, followed 
by post-graduate training in many countries 
including the UK, Israel, The United States and 
Canada.
He has served on many national, provincial and 
health organization’s committees and boards 
including the Canadian National Advisory Council 
on Aging, the Ontario Drug Benefit Program’s 
Quality and Therapeutics Committee. He continues 
to serve on the the College of Physicians of Ontario, 
and on the Provincial Coroner’s Committee on 
Geriatrics and Long-Term.
His books include his first book Old Enough to 
Feel Better: A Medical Guide for Seniors which went 
through three editions; An Ounce of Prevention: A 
medical guide for a healthy and successful retirement; 
The Encyclopedia of Health and Aging; Parenting 
your Parents (two Canadian and one American 
edition); Brooklyn Beginnings: A Geriatrician’s 
Odyssey; Moments that Matter: Cases in Ethical 
Eldercare; and most recently Late-Stage Dementia: 
Promoting Comfort Compassion and Care and now 
the revised third edition of Parenting your Parents: 
Straight Talk about Aging in the Family.

Toni Janik, WRH Windsor Regional Hospital

Digital economy

CANADA, LIBRARIES, &  
THE DIGITAL ECONOMY

Catherine Middleton, Professor, Ted 
Rogers School of Management

Governments have promised a Digital 
Economy Strategy for years but Canada is still lagging in 
technology adoption and broadband access. How can we 
convince governments to change this and to see libraries 
as an enabler of the knowledge economy? What role can 
libraries play to foster innovation, connectedness, and 
access to technology in an increasingly competitive world?
Learn about what a digital economy strategy might mean, 
how Canada is falling behind its competitors, what progress 
has been made, how you can help communicate the 
urgency and importance of this, examples of “intelligent 
communities” and ultra-fast broadband projects all over the 
world. And learn how you, your library, and your community 
can take advantage of this growing movement.

Todd Kyle, Newmarket PL

Maker culture, Media arts

INTERACTIVE MAKER ART BUILDS 
COMMUNITY COHESIVENESS

Alan Groombridge, New Media Artist;  
Rachel Martin, Innisfil PL

Bring wonder and imagination into the 
library with an unexpected flavour by 
integrating collaborative, new media art 
projects that will spark the creativity of 
the entire community! Individuals with 
innovative talents, unique skills, and fresh 
ideas have been tinkering, making and 
interacting in IPL’s public spaces…and 
now we invite you to join us! Participants 
will leave with ideas and inspiration for 
initiating collaborative maker projects, which mesh together 
innovation, art and technology. IPL will demonstrate 
successful strategies to engage the community and build 
cohesiveness through curiosity provoking, inclusive 
experiences in this hands-on workshop of fun, exploration 
and invention!
PS Come prepared to play!

Jayne Asselstine, Innisfil PL
