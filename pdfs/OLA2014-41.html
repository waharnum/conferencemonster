  INFORMATION SEEKING  

EXPERIENCES OF DISTANCE LEARNERS

Nancy E. Black, Executive Director, Library 
Services, Nipissing University

Based on Ms. Black’s study conducted towards the 
completion of her doctoral dissertation: Information 
Seeking Experiences of post-secondary distance students. 
The poster will include: research problem; methodology 
(hermeneutic phenomenology); analysis; selected findings; 
conclusion/recommendations. While there were some 
expected findings (such as a strong preference for the use 
of Google for information seeking) there were unexpected 
findings as well, such as the significance of time as an 
influencing factor when searching which was strongly linked 
to levels of motivation for searching and domain knowledge 
of the subject matter.

  SYSTEMATIC SEARCH  

STRATEGIES AS A RUBRIC FOR RESEARCHERS

Heather Cunningham, Gerstein Science Information Centre; 
Carla Hagstrom, Library Instruction Co-ordinator, Gerstein 
Science Information Centre; University of Toronto

A universe of possibilities is what researchers face 
when conducting a comprehensive systematic literature 
review. The Cochrane Collaboration, gold standard for 
evidence-based research in health care interventions, 
celebrated its twentieth anniversary in 2013. We analyzed 
the search methods for the identification of studies from 
the “Developmental, Psychosocial, & Learning Problems” 
systematic reviews to determine how closely they adhere 
to the strategies described in the Cochrane Handbook for 
Systematic Reviews of Interventions. The results will be 
presented in an infographic which can be repurposed as a 
graphical teaching tool for novice researchers.

 GREY AREAS: A SYSTEMATIC  

APPROACH TO FINDING GREY  
LITERATURE FOR SYNTHESIS RESEARCH

Allison McArthur, Library & Information Specialist; Sarah Morgan, 
Library Operations Technician; Public Health Ontario

Library Services at Public Health Ontario (PHO) supports 
library patrons undertaking synthesis research to access 
and use grey literature. The lack of traditional publishing 
structures inherent in grey literature, along with the lack 
of systematic indexing and retrieving, pose challenges to 
incorporating the use of grey literature within a transparent 
and transferable research methodology. PHO’s Library 
Services provides training to synthesis researchers to help 
them apply principles of research methodology to create a 
systematic approach to retrieval and use of grey literature.

  SERVING UP INFORMATION AT APPY HOUR

Kristen Caschera, Information Services Librarian, London PL

Appy Hour is a pilot project, designed to showcase 
the library’s reference and reader’s advisory services 
surrounding ebooks, electronic collections, and other 
resources. Patrons bring their phones, tablets, and 
other devices into the library and get help with a variety 
of questions: How do I download an ebook? Can you 
recommend an app? Why am I getting this error message? 
In addition to allowing for more intensive one-on-one help, 
Appy Hour also serves as a good way to train other staff on 
this type of reference service.

  LET’S CONNECT

Karen Bisschop, Information Services Librarian;  
Karen Clysdale, Special Projects Librarian; Peterborough PL

A summary and analysis of our Library Week campaign to 
promote the library’s social media presence on Facebook 
and Twitter. Our campaign aims to increase awareness 
and use of these tools in order to help us communicate 
with both current members and non-members in our 
community. We will use several strategies, including a 
Facebook trivia contest, and report on both quantitative 
and qualitative outcomes. Our campaign analysis will be 
highly relevant to the day-to-day needs of Ontario’s small–
medium libraries that do not have specialized marketing 
staff.

  RE-INVENTING COURSE  

RESERVE SERVICE: EXCELLENCE  
WITH ARES AT CARLETON UNIVERSITY

Joanne Rumig, Course Reserves Co-ordinator, Access 
Services, Carleton University; Cindy Wilson

In 2011, Carleton University Library started to obtain 
copyright permissions for readings in-house and, in the 
process, reinvented the university’s course reserve service. 
With a unique model and a dedicated group of staff, the 
team successfully introduced a new approach to reserves 
that was recognized with Carleton’s Service Excellence 
Award. The poster will present an overview of the project 
implementation and offers practical suggestions for those 
universities considering Ares for their library and copyright 
in a university environment.

  WHERE ARE YOU?  

A STORY OF DIGITAL GOVDOCS IN ONTARIO

Simone O’Byrne, Ontario Ministry of the Environment; 
Peter Duerr, Scott Library, York University

Over a million pages of Ontario Government Documents 
have been digitized over the past several years courtesy 
of the Ontario Digitization Initiative (ODI). The full text of 
these documents is publicly and permanently hosted at the 
University of Toronto’s Internet Archive. 
In order to establish a snapshot inventory of past, present, 
and future efforts, a 32 question survey was circulated 
among 77 institutions. Data from the 22 responses was 
collated and presented at the 2013 OCUL Summit. This 
poster will showcase the major themes of our findings.
